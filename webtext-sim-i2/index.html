Social Media and the Escalation of Real World Violence

Simran Gill

A case study of the sectarian violence in Pakistan and the 2017 Rohingya genocide in Myanmar.

More

Introduction

Sectarian violence within the Islamic world has existed for centuries with particular focus on the Shia and Sunni sects. Sectarianism is defined as “very strong support for the religious or political group that you are a member of, which can cause problems between different groups” (Cambridge Dictionary). These two sects share the same core beliefs but differ in matters such as successorship and theology (Gaiser, 2022, p. 86). The violence that stems from Islamic sectarianism mainly affects the Shia Muslims who are the minority sect of Islam. Violence against the Shia sect has always persisted, tracing back to the Battle of Karbala (Gaiser, 2022, p. 91).

The hatred spread against Shia Muslims is often done through religious figures and online extremists. These religious figures have immense influence and power depending on where they are from, for example Saudi Arabia (Human Rights Watch, 2017). These scholars use documents and rulings, which are called fatwas in Islamic terms, to justify the vilification of the Shia muslims; which directly affect the Shias living in Saudi Arabia. Their primary way to spread these hateful rhetorics are through the use of the press, television and social media.

Digital platforms in particular have become a modern amplifier for sectarian hatred. This can often be seen on social media platforms such as Facebook, X, YouTube and TikTok. This method of spreading hatred and anti-Shia rhetoric is a major shift from simply giving speeches or sermons to a group of people. Social media allows for mass consumption of these hateful rhetorics and also allows for the mass sharing of such content.

Social media algorithms allow the spread of such hateful content, however these platforms do more than just present this hatred, they take this hate and spread it widely and quickly which produces an audience that harms Shia communities and other marginalized communities. The social media algorithm filters and prioritizes which content gets pushed out—they have the power to control which messages are amplified and which are silenced (Gillespie et al., 2013).

When these algorithms amplify anti-Shia rhetoric they are pushing this content out to those who hold this prejudice and therefore recommending more of this content to them and preventing them from seeing other viewpoints. Social media platforms as a whole prioritizes content that gains the highest amount of metrics such as likes, shares and comments—because this is what leads to profit. This means that the algorithm deems the content with the highest metrics and interactions as the most relevant (Gillespie et al., 2013).

Algorithms that aim to keep people engaged often promote shocking, divisive and emotional content. This allows for sectarian messages to be spread more widely and have greater impact than ever before. As a result, this hatred is no longer limited to one place—they are spread across borders and can quickly inspire supporters and attackers.

This paper examines how social media is not simply a tool for communication but also an active force which speeds up religious hatred and mobilizes real-world violence. This paper will focus on two major case studies that demonstrate the dangerous effects of hatred spread online. The first case is sectarian violence in Pakistan and the second is the Rohingya genocide in Myanmar.

Sunnis and Shia Muslims live together in Pakistan which is made up of a majority Muslim population and is a multi-sectarian society. Social media in Pakistan has become a major tool for spreading sectarian hatred (Ali et al., 2023, p. 99). Studies have shown that platforms such as Facebook and X are often used to spread anti-Shia rhetoric (Ali et al., 2023, p. 99). Sectarian hatred and violence has been prevalent in Pakistan long before online platforms existed. Scholars argue that online hate speech plays a vital role in normalizing hostility towards Shia Muslims (Ali et al., 2023, p. 99). This hatred is not confined to online spaces, this hatred manifests in real life through harassment, targeted killing and attacks (Ali et al., 2023, p. 99).

A similar pattern can be seen in Myanmar, where social media—especially Facebook played a major role in the violence that led to the 2017 Rohingya genocide (Schissler, 2025, pp. 445-446). Schissler (2025) argues that Facebook was not simply a neutral site where hate speech happened to spread, but rather it was an active system which helped promote and normalize the anti-Rohingya violence. False information, dehumanizing messages and anti-Muslim propaganda were pushed through Facebook’s algorithms and reached huge audiences in a country where the platform was the main source of news (Schissler, 2025, p. 446).

Digital platforms have intensified sectarian hatred that fuels widespread violence. By comparing sectarian hatred in Pakistan with the 2017 Rohingya genocide in Myanmar, this paper argues that digital platforms strengthen religious hatred and contribute to real-world violence against religious minorities.

Historical Background

The origins of Sunni and Shia sectarianism goes back to the period of 632 CE after the death of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), which was a time that caused intense political and religious crisis for the early Muslim community (Gaiser, 2022, p. 32). The Prophet’s death has left the ummah (Muslim community) without a clear leader, which forced Muslims to face several questions such as who should lead after the Prophet and what authority they should have (Gaiser, 2022, p. 32).

This was not simply a matter of basic leadership issues, this shaped the idea of religious authority and rule in Islam. At this point in time clear sectarian groups had not fully developed (Gaiser, 2022, p. 32). The lack of agreement over who should be the successor of the Prophet created the first major split in Islam and began long-lasting disagreements in the years that followed (Gaiser, 2022, p. 32). These early divisions became the basis of sectarianism that still influences Muslim societies today.

Algorithms and the Pyramid of Hate

Social media platforms do more than simply reflect what users are interested in; they actively shape the information that people see, which in turn affect beliefs and actions. Gillespie (2013, p. 168) explains that algorithms in search engines, recommendation systems and social networks act as “public relevance algorithms,” which decide what content is shown and hidden. By selecting information these algorithms give visibility to certain content and filter out others, leaving users to see only select narratives (Gillespie, 2013, pp. 167-168). In sectarian situations such as the anti-Shia rhetoric or the anti-Rohingya propaganda in Myanmar—this algorithmic sorting can push divisive and dehumanizing content, leaving a digital space where extreme views are normalized and accepted.

The concept of “calculated publics” further increases the power of algorithms (Gillespie, 2013, p. 168). Gillespie (2013) explains that algorithms show information to groups in ways that shape how they see themselves and what they deem important, promoting some voices while limiting opposing ones (p. 168). Within these algorithm shaped communities, people often see content that matches what they already think, which makes their views feel justified and gives them a sense of social approval for prejudiced behaviour.

This pattern can also be explained through the “pyramid of hate” framework proposed by Syed and Ali (2021). At the bottom of the pyramid are “biased attitudes,” which appear as stereotypes, subtle insults and offensive language in everyday life (Syed & Ali, 2021, p. 45). If these attitudes are not challenged they can form into more open “acts of bias,” such as bullying or harmful jokes, then into discrimination in schools, workplaces or communities—and eventually into violence aimed at individuals or targeted attacks on places of worship (Syed & Ali, 2021, p. 45).

At the top of the pyramid and at its most extreme level, this process can lead to genocide, where entire communities are targeted for destruction (Syed & Ali, 2021, p. 45). When hate speech is spread and boosted by algorithms, it speeds up movement up this pyramid by normalizing prejudice and hatred, shaping how groups view each other and encouraging hostile actions. Individuals belonging to minority groups often face greater risk, showing how online platforms contribute to real-world harm (Syed & Ali, 2021, p. 45).

Case Study: Sectarian Violence in Pakistan

Understanding anti-Shia violence in Pakistan begins with recognizing how hate speech can spark wider patterns of discrimination and sectarian harm. The global spread of hate speech is connected to social media, whose design allows harmful messages to move quickly and without much oversight (Trajova & Neshkovaska, 2019, as cited in Ali et al., 2023, p. 100). Pakistan shows this pattern clearly; the country has a long history of religious violence and sectarian hostility, especially towards Shia Muslims, and continues to appear in both physical and online spaces (Sarfraz, 2017; Ali & David, 2021, as cited in Ali et al., 2023, p. 100). In this setting, anti-Shia rhetoric and hate speech on social media does more than reflect existing prejudice, it feeds into a cycle of bias and violence that puts Shia communities in danger.

Ali et al. 2023 provide examples of threats against Shias circulating on Pakistani Facebook pages, such statements include “He is a Shia boy. He is spreading propaganda. Just see his snap shots. He should be killed (slander)” as well as “Ohhonourlesswe will destroy your generations. We will kill you” (p. 106). The authors classify these posts as sectarian hate speech and as a direct incitement to murder or even genocide: “These threats of murder equate with real murder” (Ali et al., 2023, p. 106). This shows how online hate speech and rhetoric is tied to real world sectarian killings in Pakistan.

Another article written by Syed Mujtaba Haider, demonstrates the impact of digital platforms on sectarian hatred in Pakistan, especially in the way online spaces have shaped political and religious identities of young people. Studies show that the most active creators and sharers of sectarian hate speech in Pakistan are young men between 15 and 30 (Syed, 2025, p. 36). Many of these users see social media as a source of meaning that gives them purpose and identity, as one 22-year old from Jhang explained: “It gave me a purpose, Now I know who the real enemies of Islam are” (Syed, 2025, p. 36). This statement shows how digital platforms can act as tools of radicalization and quickly spread hateful rhetoric to others.

Much of this radicalization happens through the social identity of young people online (Syed, 2025, p. 36). Youth from madrassah (Islamic school) backgrounds, who are already exposed to strict sectarian teachings, often rely on Whatsapp PDFs for “authentic” knowledge (Syed, 2025, p. 36). Scholars who promote peace are dismissed as being “liberalized,” while divisive messages receive greater visibility (Syed, 2025, p. 36). This trend fits into Social Identity Theory, “where group affiliation shapes perception and justifies exclusion or hostility toward others” (Syed, 2025, p. 36).

Online anonymity encourages young people to share extremist views and opinions where in person they might avoid expressing, “contributing to what scholars have termed ‘cyber-militancy’” (Syed, 2025, pp. 36-37). Due to platforms offering few barriers, these sectarian and violent narratives spread quickly and easily allowing extreme ideologies to become widely accepted.

Algorithms that are built to maximize engagement push out the most emotional and polarizing content. Hateful posts with dramatic titles such as “Enemies of Ahl-e-Bait (The family of the Prophet) in Pakistan” or “Yazeed’s Followers Today”, spread quickly and gain massive visibility (Syed, 2025, p. 38).

Another negative aspect of such algorithms, specially the moderation, is that it fails to detect these narratives in languages such as Urdu, Punjabi, and Pashto (Syed, 2025, p. 38). Platform AI cannot reliably understand these languages, leading many harmful posts and hateful content to spread without any checks (Syed, 2025, p. 38). In the cases where users report such content, responses are slow or inconsistent, leading people to lose trust in online safety measures (Syed, 2025, p. 38).

Digital platforms act as fuel for violence, online narratives can directly push people toward violence. Several major incidents over the past decade demonstrate how misinformation, and sectarian anger can lead to real-world violence. One early and well-known example is the case of Junaid Hafeez, a university lecturer who was arrested in 2013 in southern Punjab after fake Facebook posts falsely accused him of blasphemy (Syed, 2025, p. 39). Even though the posts were later shown to be fabricated, Hafeez’s lawyer, Rashid Rehman who agreed to defend him, was murdered by extremists (Syed, 2025, p. 39). This case is one of many showing how online accusations can endanger those in real life.

Case Study: The Rohingya Genocide and Facebook in Myanmar

The Rohingya genocide is often mentioned as a clear example of how digital platforms can help fuel mass violence. In Myanmar, Facebook became so widespread that it became synonymous with the internet itself, as Hesam Nourooz Pour notes. “for the majority of Myanmar’s 20 million internet-connected citizens, Facebook is the internet” (Nourooz, 2023, p. 95). This shows how deeply the platform shapes people’s access to information.

Facebook’s role in Myanmar’s online life made it easy for digital hate to translate into real-world violence. Government backed groups and Buddhist nationalists used the platforms to spread dehumanizing claims about Rohingya Muslims that labeled them as “illegal immigrants,” and “terrorists” (Nourooz, 2023, pp. 95-96). This propaganda created a moral panic that the military used to justify raids and crackdowns (Nourooz, 2023, p. 96).

This case shows how powerful social media platforms can be in shaping political and social changes. Nourooz explains that platforms like Facebook have contributed to many major global events, in both positive and negative ways (Nourooz, 2023, p. 96). In some regions social media platforms have helped strengthen digital literacy, but in other cases such as Youtube’s role during the Yazidi genocide and Facebook’s role in the Rohingya genocide, “illustrate how digital platforms can significantly inflame national, ethnic and religious prejudices, incite violence and disseminate disinformation (Nourooz, 2023, p. 98).

Shared Patterns of Online Hatred: Pakistan and Myanmar

Both Pakistan and Myanmar show how online hatred can turn into real-world violence when digital platforms fail to control extremist content. The examples documented by Ali et al. (2023) in Pakistan resemble many of the dynamics seen in Myanmar during the Rohingya genocide. Ali et al. (2023) showed that Facebook posts in Pakistan openly called for Shia Muslims to be killed, wiped out or be treated as “infidels” and that this online hatred often led to real attacks (p. 106). Syed’s article (2025) demonstrates how sectarianism on social media can create extremely dangerous situations for people in real life, where fake social media posts accusing others of blasphemy leads to imprisonment and executions (p. 39-41).

This dynamic was also seen in Myanmar where Facebook was used to spread hate speech, lies, and dehumanizing rhetoric about Rohingya Muslims (Nourooz, 2023). Due to the fact that Facebook was the main source of information for millions of people, the online hatred allowed for the mass violence of Muslims (Nourooz, 2023, p. 95). Comparing these two case studies shows the underlying pattern is the same: digital platforms amplify hate and normalize violence against religious minorities.
